▸ How this is calculated

Both choices buy the same goods at the same times — your visits don’t change with how you pay — so consumption cancels and the cheaper financing wins. We compare the present value (PV) of cash leaving your pocket.

The two cash flows

Prepay: pay X once, receive a balance B = X + Y, draw it down at m per month. PV of outflow = X.

Stay invested: keep X compounding at r, pay m/month out of pocket for the same goods until B is spent — that’s L = (X+Y)/m months of payments.

i = (1+r)^(1/12) − 1  (monthly rate)
PV_invest = Σ over months k=0… of min(m, remaining) ÷ (1+i)^k

Decision rule, with net value today Δ:

Δ = PV_invest − X  →  prepay if Δ > 0

Break-even = the prepayment’s effective yield

The return r* that drives Δ to zero is the annual yield the prepaid dollars effectively earn (their IRR). If your index fund is expected to beat r*, invest; if not, prepay. A quick mental estimate (the tool solves it exactly):

r* ≈ 2d ÷ T_yr ,  d = Y/(X+Y) ,  T_yr = (X+Y)/(12m)

You capture discount d on money tied up for, on average, half the drawdown horizon — hence the factor of 2. The dominant lever is deposit size relative to burn rate (T_yr), not the headline bonus.

Why there’s no separate inflation input

Discounting nominal cash flows at the nominal return r already captures inflation — r includes inflation compensation, so Δ is in today’s purchasing power. A second inflation haircut would double-count. (This holds because m is a fixed nominal amount.) Use your after-tax expected return, since the prepayment’s “return” isn’t taxed but the index fund’s is.

What this ignores (all push toward investing)

  • Breakage & expiry: unused or expiring credit is a dead loss not modeled here.
  • Counterparty risk: a prepaid balance is an unsecured claim — if the store fails, it can vanish.
  • Lock-in: the money can only be spent there; no flexibility if your tastes or prices change.
  • Behavioral drift: a prepaid balance tends to nudge spending up; assume your m is genuinely unchanged.
  • Return uncertainty: r is an expectation, not a guarantee — the chart shows sensitivity across the whole range.

One factor cuts the other way: if you expect the store’s prices to rise, prepaying at today’s prices hedges that, modestly improving the case for it.
